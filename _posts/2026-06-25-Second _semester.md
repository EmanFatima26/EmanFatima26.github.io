Armed with a satisfying GPA from my first semester, I entered the second semester with a renewed sense of enthusiasm and energy. My mind was completely made up: I wanted to put all my focus into programming, polish my coding skills, and absorb as many new technical abilities as possible. As classes commenced, we discovered a shift in our faculty—our HOD, Sir Akram, took over Programming Fundamentals, while our absolute favorite, **Dr. Bilal**, was assigned to teach us Database Systems. Knowing his expertise in Machine Learning (ML), Deep Learning (DL), and database design, we knew we were in for an incredibly insightful ride.

### Diving into Databases and Facing the OOP Challenge

Right from the start, Dr. Bilal aligned his lectures with a practical vision: he wanted us to build fully functional websites. The first few weeks flew by in downloading, installing, and setting up WampServer, MySQL Workbench, and SQL Server in the labs. Simultaneously, we introduced ourselves to Object-Oriented Programming (OOP) using C#. Coming from a Python background without ever studying C++, the strict syntax of C# felt like a mountain to climb, though it gradually started making sense over time.

However, life outside academics began to shift dramatically. Friendships evolved, and I started facing severe health issues. Every time I traveled back to the hostel, I would come down with a high fever, which badly hit my university attendance. Between the academic pressure and the terrible hostel mess food, I developed severe stomach issues, resulting in noticeable weight loss. Balancing physical sickness with a demanding semester was turning into my biggest test.

### The Midnight Portfolio Crisis & A Helping Hand from Gemini

Our database curriculum was intense. By the mid-terms, Dr. Bilal had taught us how to connect a website’s front-end with a dataset. Initially, the plan was to build a real-time product website and purchase a domain, but due to financial constraints and student concerns, Sir graciously modified the project scope. We focused heavily on database designing, briefly mastering normalization techniques (1NF, 2NF, and 3NF) to eliminate data redundancy and anomalies. 

The real drama unfolded when Dr. Bilal tasked us with creating a repository-based portfolio on GitHub. The submission deadline was a chaotic Sunday night—right before our crucial OOP final exam the next morning! Instead of preparing for the exam, my friend and I found ourselves staring blankly at our screens over the weekend, completely lost because previous backlogs had left us unprepared. To make matters worse, my Wi-Fi stopped working. After begging a friend to let me connect to their ID, I finally sat down, opened Gemini, and started asking step-by-step questions: *"What am I supposed to do here? How does this work?"* 

By 4:00 AM, my portfolio was up, but it looked terrible. Refusing to submit a subpar project, I kept tweaking the code, changing the theme styles, and restructuring the layout according to my taste until it was finally perfect at 6:00 AM. Meanwhile, my friend was in tears out of pure panic. I took a deep breath, sat with her, and guided her through the entire process. By the grace of Almighty, we both submitted our portfolios right on time.

### The Mid-Term Slump and the 5-Step Dataset Approval

Our mid-terms happened right after a stretch of online classes. Due to recurring internet issues and a bit of my own laziness, I had missed several lectures. Online coordination never matches the clarity of physical classes, so the mid-terms felt incredibly difficult and didn't go as well as I hoped. But as they say, tough times pass. When the results slowly rolled out, destiny surprised me—the marks were way better than my expectations!

After the mids, the real grind began. We dived deep into actual ML model training and front-end connection. I still vividly remember a lab day when the electricity went out right before our session; we all celebrated, thinking we were free, only for the power to return at the last minute, forcing us back to work! 

Dr. Bilal set a dedicated lab just for project dataset approvals. My group partner and I chose a dataset and went up for evaluation. Sir asked a few conceptual questions, we fumbled, and he sent us back. We studied again and went back—rejected again. It took us **five attempts** of studying, revising, and representing before Dr. Bilal finally gave us the green light. But by that fifth time, we knew our dataset inside out. That is the beauty of Dr. Bilal’s teaching—he uses a friendly approach to encourage students, and his knowledge is so vast that he knows exactly how to push a student until they truly understand the concept.

### Two Back-to-Back Exhibitions: DLD and Databases

As the final weeks approached, quizzes, lab vivas, and project deadlines hit us simultaneously. The hardest hurdle was our Digital Logic Design (DLD) project, which required building a physical working circuit. We had to travel 45 minutes away to Faisalabad just to buy the electronic components. On our first trip, some components turned out to be missing, and on the second trip, they weren't available. Frustrated, we scrapped our original token-system project idea and pivoted to a sensor-based counter system. 

After two days of relentless manual labor, I ended up assembling the entire project on Sunday. It wasn't working perfectly, and the exhibition was the next morning! We pulled another all-night grind, and by sunrise, it was at least functioning better than a dead circuit. The DLD exhibition went smoothly, but the battle wasn't over.

The very next day was our Database Project Exhibition. We bunked our regular lectures, locked ourselves away to write the project reports, and re-verified our dataset’s GUI. During the exhibition, seniors and faculty swarmed our tables, firing questions. Ultimately, Dr. Bilal announced that our final evaluation would depend on a professionally recorded project video. 

### Final Exams & Final Reflections: Thank You, Dr. Bilal

Our final exam routine was a hectic sprint—papers back-to-back without a single preparatory holiday. Some subjects required starting entirely from scratch. But amidst the chaos, I received the shock of the semester: **I was in the top 7 students for Database Systems!** I was ecstatic and surprised. This achievement belonged entirely to Dr. Bilal, who cleared our database concepts so deeply that the logic stuck with us. Though the final Database exam was highly challenging and tested our limits, it went reasonably well. On the final day of exams, we shot our project video, edited it, and submitted it.

Looking back at this intense second semester, I realize how much Dr. Bilal invested in us. He dedicated his time, energy, and unmatched knowledge to make us capable students. I often look at his LinkedIn profile for inspiration; his personal journey is a massive motivating factor for me. Everything I have learned about real-life problem solving, database architecture, and machine learning would have been impossible without him. I am profoundly thankful to him. 

The second semester ended with a massive gain in knowledge and a folder full of overcome challenges. I truly hope we get the honor of being taught by Dr. Bilal again in our upcoming semesters. Onward and upward!
